How to Actually Build the Pneumatic Bladder

From a syringe on your desk to a soft pad under the cane handle — the trapped air is the sensor. The barometer just reads how hard it's squeezed.

Time: ~1 hour Cost: under $5 of parts Tools: hot glue + scissors Sensor: LPS22HB barometer

00 First: feel it with zero electronics

Before wiring anything, build the mental model with something already in your kitchen.

The whole idea in one sentence

Seal some air in a soft container. When you press it, the air can't escape — so it pushes back, and its pressure rises in exact proportion to how hard you press.

A balloon, a zip-lock bag, or a capped syringe all work. Squeeze a sealed bag and feel it resist — that resistance is a pressure you can measure. The barometer is just a tiny gauge that reads that pressure as a number. That's the entire sensor.

The complete sensor. Force on the pouch → trapped air pressure rises → barometer reads a number. No moving electronics, no force chip.

01 Shopping list (all cheap, all easy)

Plastic syringe

10–20 ml, no needle. The "desk proof" version + a perfect calibration piston.

Balloon / silicone pouch

The soft bladder. A thick party balloon or a small heat-sealed TPU pouch under the grip.

Silicone / aquarium tube

~3 mm bore, 20–40 cm. Carries pressure from bladder to sensor. Soft silicone seals best.

Nano 33 BLE

You have this. LPS22HB barometer is already on the board — no extra chip.

Hot glue / epoxy

The make-or-break. Every joint must be air-tight. Silicone sealant also works.

Bathroom scale

Your reference truth. Used once, to calibrate barometer counts → kilograms.

02 Build it in two stages

Stage A proves the electronics work on your desk in 15 minutes. Stage B is the real thing on the cane. Do A first — it removes all the guesswork.

Stage A · desk proof

The syringe sensor

Pull the plunger to trap air, glue the nozzle to the tube, glue the other end over the barometer inside a tiny sealed cap. Push the plunger → watch kPa climb. If this works, the physics is proven.
Stage B · on the cane

The handle bladder

Same circuit, soft pad instead of a syringe. A big soft bladder under the grip spreads body weight over ~30 cm² so the pressure lands inside the barometer's readable window.
Why a big soft pad, not a small hard piston:   P = F / A pressure = force ÷ area
35 kgf on a 19 mm cane tip (2.8 cm²) → 1,200 kPa — bursts everything ✗
35 kgf on a 30 cm² handle pad      → ~110 kPa, soft-wall absorbs most → lands in range ✓

03 Step-by-step assembly

  1. Trap the air Pull the syringe plunger halfway (Stage A), or just leave normal air in the soft bladder (Stage B). This trapped air is your spring — don't squeeze it all out.
  2. Connect the tube Push the silicone tube firmly onto the syringe nozzle / bladder neck. Add a ring of hot glue around the joint. Air leaks here are the #1 failure — over-seal it.
  3. Make the sensor cap Sit the barometer over the LPS22HB. Build a tiny sealed pocket over the sensor port (a syringe-tip cap or 3D-printed manifold) and feed the tube into it. Glue airtight, keeping the dead air space small.
  4. Seal everything & leak-test Press the bladder and hold. If the kPa reading slowly falls back to zero, you have a leak — chase it with more glue until a squeeze holds steady for 10+ seconds.
  5. Mount on the cane (Stage B) Tuck the bladder under the handle grip, run the tube down the shaft to the Nano in a small housing near the bottom. Keep the tube from kinking.
  6. Calibrate against a scale Stand the cane tip on a bathroom scale, press to known weights, log the pairs (see below), fit a line. Now barometer counts read out in kilograms.

04 The IMU fixes the one weakness — for free

Sealed air drifts: a warm hand, sun, or a tiny leak slowly shifts the zero. You don't fight this with better sealing — you let the IMU re-zero the baseline every step, during the swing phase when the cane is in the air and carrying zero load.

Free drift cancellation. The IMU already knows when the cane is swinging (load = 0). Re-zero there each step → temperature, creep, and slow leaks all vanish.

05 Calibration: counts → kilograms

Press the cane onto a bathroom scale at a few known weights and log what the barometer reads. Fit a straight line through the pairs — that line converts every future reading into kg.

Scale showsBarometer readsWhat it means
0 kg (cane in air)0 kPabaseline / tare point
10 kg~3 kPalight lean
20 kg~6 kPatypical partial weight-bearing
35 kg~11 kPaheavy lean (top of range)

Numbers illustrative — yours depend on bladder area and stiffness. The point: a smooth, repeatable line. Good to ~10–20%, which already beats a therapist's 20–30% tactile guess.

Don't skip the leak test

90% of "it doesn't work" is a slow air leak at a glued joint. Squeeze, hold, and watch: a healthy sensor holds its pressure for 10+ seconds. If it bleeds down, find the hiss and re-glue before doing anything else.
