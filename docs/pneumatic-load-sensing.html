Measuring Weight-Bearing with Sensors We Already Have

Turning a barometer into a load sensor.

Sensors in hand: LSM9DS1 IMU + LPS22HB barometer No FSR · No load cell · $0 BOM add Target load: ~7–35 kgf (70–340 N)
The constraint we're boxed into

We have only two sensors and can't buy more: an LSM9DS1 IMU and an LPS22HB barometer (range 260–1260 hPa, ~1 Pa RMS noise, ≤75 Hz) on an Arduino Nano 33 BLE. No FSR, no load cell. Yet the goal is to measure weight-bearing / loading compliance — how much body weight (~7–35 kgf / 70–340 N) a person transfers through the cane. A barometer measures air pressure. So how do we weigh a human with a weather sensor?

The reframe — we don't need precision, we need a repeatable trend

Weight-bearing compliance does not need absolute precision.

Clinical targets are deliberately coarse — "30–50% body weight", "20 kg ± 10 kg". And the standard we actually compete against — a therapist's tactile guess — is wrong by 20–30%. So a repeatable, relative load trend accurate to ~10–20% already matches or beats clinical practice. We are not building a scale; we are building a compliance coach.

01The core idea — a pneumatic load cell

Turn the barometer into a pneumatic load cell using the oldest trick in pressure sensing: P = F / A.

A soft sealed air bladder (~25–35 cm² footprint — a silicone dome or a heat-sealed TPU pouch) sits under the handle grip. Body weight on the grip compresses the bladder, so the trapped air pressure rises. A thin silicone tube pipes that pressure down to the LPS22HB, sealed in a small 3D-printed manifold. The barometer reads the pressure; a calibration curve maps it to kgf.

Hero cross-section. Body weight (orange) compresses the bladder under the grip; the trapped-air pressure (cyan) travels down a silicone tube to the sealed barometer manifold near the tip.

02Why under the handle, not at the tip

The obvious instinct — put a piston in the ferrule — runs straight into the barometer's range. The geometry forces the bladder up to the handle.

The LPS22HB has a usable working window of about 25 kPa above ambient before it saturates. To squeeze the full 35 kgf into that window, the contact area has to be large:

A = F / P = 340 N / 25 000 Pa ≈ 136 cm²  (a ~13 cm piston) P = F / A, solved for area

A 13 cm piston is impossible inside a 19 mm ferrule. Forcing those 340 N through the ~2.8 cm² of a tip piston instead would demand pressures on the order of ~MPa — that bursts the chamber and instantly saturates the sensor. The fix is counter-intuitive: a large soft bladder under the handle, operated near the bottom of the pressure range, with per-step auto-tare. The big footprint keeps the pressure in-window; the soft wall avoids the rigid-piston pressure spike.

Approach A · rejected

✕ Rigid piston in the ferrule

  • Only ~2.8 cm² of area at a 19 mm tip → needs ~MPa pressure for 35 kgf.
  • Bursts the chamber and saturates instantly — the LPS22HB tops out at ~1260 hPa.
  • A rigid piston also transmits shock spikes, not a smooth load signal.
Approach B · shipping

✓ Soft bladder under the handle

  • Large ~25–35 cm² footprint keeps pressure inside the 25 kPa window.
  • Operate at the bottom of the range + per-step auto-tare → buildable headroom.
  • Soft silicone/TPU wall spreads load smoothly — no burst, no spike.

03The IMU's secret weapon — auto-tare every step

A sealed gas chamber drifts — and the drift is as big as the signal. The IMU cancels it for free.

Hand heat warms the trapped air, the elastomer creeps under sustained load, and there are inevitable micro-leaks. Left alone, the baseline wanders by an amount comparable to the weight-bearing reading itself. The fix: the IMU already detects the gait swing phase — the cane is in the air, so the load is exactly zero. Re-zero (tare) the barometer baseline at that instant, every single gait cycle. One free move cancels thermal drift, creep, and leak together.

Gait-cycle auto-tare. The barometer baseline is re-zeroed during every swing phase (green), so thermal drift, creep, and leaks never accumulate into the stance-phase reading (amber).

04Calibration — a bathroom scale and a curve fit

Mapping barometer counts to kgf is a one-time bench step, the same method instrumented-crutch researchers use against force plates:

  1. Stand the cane on a bathroom scale. The scale gives ground-truth kgf.
  2. Press to a set of known loads. Walk it up the range — 5, 10, 20, 30 kgf.
  3. Log barometer counts vs scale reading at each known load.
  4. Fit the curve (linear or mild polynomial) → counts become kgf on-device.

Design concept · not a medical device
Moon Walk — pneumatic load-sensing approach for weight-bearing compliance. This document describes an engineering concept for a consumer-wellness device intended to surface relative loading trends, not to provide diagnostic or clinical-grade force measurements. It is not a medical device and must not be relied upon for medical decisions.
